## Option A - Question: "What is the most accurate way to estimate one-rep max without testing a true max?"

**Post this as an answer:**

---

Use a formula stack instead of relying on a single equation.

Most people only use one formula (usually Epley), but accuracy varies with rep range and fatigue. A better approach is to calculate Brzycki + Epley + Lombardi, then use the average.

Core formulas for weight `w` and reps `r`:

- Brzycki: `w / (1.0278 - 0.0278 * r)`
- Epley: `w * (1 + r / 30)`
- Lombardi: `w * r^0.10`

Then:

```
consensus = (brzycki + epley + lombardi) / 3
```

You get a more stable estimate because one formula's bias gets dampened by the other two.

Practical tip: use hard sets in the 3-5 rep range for the cleanest signal.

## Option B - Question: "How do I choose warm-up sets before heavy squats or deadlifts?"

**Post this as an answer:**

---

Use a ramp based on a current estimated 1RM, not random jumps.

Simple structure:

1. Empty bar x 8-10
2. ~40% x 6-8
3. ~55% x 4-5
4. ~70% x 2-3
5. ~80% x 1-2
6. ~90% x 1

Then start work sets.

That progression gives enough neural activation while keeping fatigue low.

Most warm-up mistakes are either:

- Too few sets (first work set feels shockingly heavy)
- Too many reps near working load (you pre-fatigue before the real work)

## Option C - Question: "How should I pick attempts for my first powerlifting meet?"

**Post this as an answer:**

---

Default to conservative early attempts.

A reliable first-pass structure:

- Opener: ~90%
- Second: ~97%
- Third: ~101-103% if bar speed and confidence are there

Your opener should be something you can hit even on a mediocre day.

Reason: if you miss opener, the entire meet gets harder psychologically and strategically.

For new lifters, going 8/9 or 9/9 with smart attempt jumps is usually better than chasing one risky number too early.

## Option D - Question: "How do I convert a target 1RM into working weight for sets of 5?"

**Post this as an answer:**

---

Use reverse calculation.

Instead of entering lifted weight + reps to estimate max, reverse mode starts with target 1RM and reps, then back-calculates likely working weight.

That is useful when building a block from a known max.

Example flow:

1. Enter target 1RM (say 315)
2. Enter reps (say 5)
3. Reverse-calc returns working weight estimates (Brzycki/Epley/Lombardi + consensus)

From there, round to your plate setup and apply progression week-to-week.

## Option E - Question: "How can you test your muscular strength?"

**Post this as an answer:**

---

The simplest way is to test the heaviest load you can move with good form on a standard lift.

Three practical options:

1. True 1-rep max (1RM) test
2. Estimated 1RM from a hard 3 to 6 rep set
3. Standardized strength tests like handgrip dynamometer plus key barbell lifts

For most people, option 2 is the best mix of safety and usefulness.

How to do it well:

- Pick a consistent lift (squat, bench, deadlift, overhead press)
- Use the same technique standards every time
- Warm up progressively before the test set
- Record weight, reps, and bar speed or form quality

If you do not want to max out directly, use a rep-max set and estimate your 1RM with a formula. That gives you a reliable strength benchmark without the fatigue and risk of frequent true max attempts.

I usually use a formula stack (Brzycki, Epley, Lombardi) and average them, because one formula alone can skew high or low depending on rep range.

Retest every 4 to 8 weeks under similar conditions so your numbers are comparable.

## Option F - Question: "How do you calibrate number of reps/sets/amount of weight in a strength training program?"

**Post this as an answer:**

---

Calibrate in this order: goal, intensity, reps, sets, progression.

Most people do the reverse and pick random sets/reps first, which is why progress stalls.

### 1) Pick one primary goal per block

Use one focus for 4 to 8 weeks:

- Max strength
- Hypertrophy with strength carryover
- Work capacity and technical practice

### 2) Set load from a current strength anchor

Use a tested or estimated 1RM.

General guide:

- 85 to 92%: heavy strength
- 75 to 85%: strength plus hypertrophy
- 65 to 75%: higher-volume practice

### 3) Match reps to intensity

- 85%+: 1 to 5 reps
- 75 to 85%: 4 to 8 reps
- 65 to 75%: 6 to 12 reps

### 4) Add enough sets for productive volume

Typical starting ranges per lift per session:

- Main barbell lift: 3 to 6 work sets
- Secondary lift: 2 to 5 sets
- Accessories: 2 to 4 sets each

### 5) Use a clear progression rule

Examples:

- Add 2.5 to 5 lb when all target reps are completed with clean form
- Or hold weight and add reps until top of range, then increase load
- Or run weekly percentage waves

### 6) Regulate by performance

If bar speed drops hard or technique degrades, reduce load 2.5 to 7% or remove 1 to 2 sets.

If everything moves well for 2+ weeks, increase stress gradually.

### 7) Recalibrate every 4 to 6 weeks

Update your estimated 1RM from recent hard sets and reset percentages.

If you never recalibrate, your program gets misaligned as you get stronger.
